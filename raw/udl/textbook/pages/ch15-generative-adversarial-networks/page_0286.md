## 15.2.5 Wasserstein distance for continuous distributions

Translating these results back to the continuous multi-dimensional domain, the equivalent of the primal form (equation 15.10) is:

Problems 15.4–15.5

$$
\begin{aligned}
D_{w}\Big[Pr(\mathbf{x}),q(\mathbf{x})\Big]=\min_{\pi[\bullet,\bullet]}\left[\iint\pi(\mathbf{x}_{1},\mathbf{x}_{2})\cdot||\mathbf{x}_{1}-\mathbf{x}_{2}||d\mathbf{x}_{1}d\mathbf{x}_{2}\right],
\end{aligned}
\tag{15.14}
$$

subject to constraints similar to equation 15.11 on the transport plan $\pi(\mathbf{x}_{1}, \mathbf{x}_{2})$ representing the mass moved from position $\mathbf{x}_{1}$ to $\mathbf{x}_{2}$. The equivalent of the dual form (equation 15.12) is:

Appendix B.1.1 Lipschitz constant

$$
\begin{aligned}
D_{w}\Big[Pr(\mathbf{x}),q(\mathbf{x})\Big]=\max_{\mathrm{f}[\mathbf{x}]}\left[\int Pr(\mathbf{x})\mathrm{f}[\mathbf{x}]d\mathbf{x}-\int q(\mathbf{x})f[\mathbf{x}]d\mathbf{x}\right],
\end{aligned}
\tag{15.15}
$$

subject to the constraint that the Lipschitz constant of the function f[x] is less than one (i.e., the absolute gradient of the function is less than one).

## 15.2.6 Wasserstein GAN loss function

In the context of neural networks, we maximize over the space of functions f[x] by optimizing the parameters  $\phi$  in a neural network f[x,  $\phi$ ], and we approximate these integrals using generated samples  $x_{i}^{*}$  and real examples  $x_{i}$ :

$$
\begin{aligned}L[\phi]\quad&=\quad\sum_{j}\mathrm{f}[\mathbf{x}_{j}^{*},\boldsymbol{\phi}]-\sum_{i}\mathrm{f}[\mathbf{x}_{i},\boldsymbol{\phi}]\\&=\quad\sum_{j}\mathrm{f}[\mathbf{g}[z_{j},\boldsymbol{\theta}],\boldsymbol{\phi}]-\sum_{i}\mathrm{f}[\mathbf{x}_{i},\boldsymbol{\phi}],\end{aligned}
\tag{15.16}
$$

where we must constrain the neural network discriminator  $f[x,\phi]$  to have an absolute gradient norm of less than one at every position x:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|\frac{\partial\mathrm{f}[\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol{\phi}]}{\partial\mathbf{x}}\right|<1.
\end{aligned}
\tag{15.17}
$$

One way to achieve this is to clip the discriminator weights to a small range (e.g.,  $\pm0.01$ ). An alternative is the gradient penalty Wasserstein GAN or WGAN-GP, which adds a regularization term that increases as the gradient norm deviates from unity.
