The expectation (mean) E[f_{i}'] of the intermediate values f_{i}' is:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\begin{align*}\mathbb{E}[f_{i}^{\prime}]&=&\quad\mathbb{E}\left[\beta_{i}+\sum_{j=1}^{D_{h}}\Omega_{ij}h_{j}\right]\\&=&\quad\mathbb{E}\left[\beta_{i}\right]+\sum_{j=1}^{D_{h}}\mathbb{E}\left[\Omega_{ij}h_{j}\right]\\&=&\quad\mathbb{E}\left[\beta_{i}\right]+\sum_{j=1}^{D_{h}}\mathbb{E}\left[\Omega_{ij}\right]\mathbb{E}\left[h_{j}\right]\\&=&\quad0+\sum_{j=1}^{D_{h}}0\cdot\mathbb{E}\left[h_{j}\right]=0,\end{align*} \tag{7.29}
\end{aligned}
$$

where $D_{h}$ is the dimensionality of the input layer $\mathbf{h}$. We have used the rules for manipulating expectations, and we have assumed that the distributions over the hidden units $h_{j}$ and the network weights $\Omega_{ij}$ are independent between the second and third lines.

Appendix C.2.1

Using this result, we see that the variance $\sigma_{f}^{2}$, of the pre-activations $f_{i}^{\prime}$ is:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\begin{align*}\sigma_{f_{i}}^{2}&=\mathbb{E}[f_{i}^{\prime2}]-\mathbb{E}[f_{i}^{^{\prime}2}]\\&=\mathbb{E}\left[\left(\beta_{i}+\sum_{j=1}^{D_{h}}\Omega_{ij}h_{j}\right)^{2}\right]-0\\&=\mathbb{E}\left[\left(\sum_{j=1}^{D_{h}}\Omega_{ij}h_{j}\right)^{2}\right]\\&=\sum_{j=1}^{D_{h}}\mathbb{E}\left[\Omega_{ij}^{2}\right]\mathbb{E}\left[h_{j}^{2}\right]\\&=\sum_{j=1}^{D_{h}}\sigma_{\Omega}^{2}\mathbb{E}\left[h_{j}^{2}\right]=\sigma_{\Omega}^{2}\sum_{j=1}^{D_{h}}\mathbb{E}\left[h_{j}^{2}\right],\end{align*} \tag{7.30}
\end{aligned}
$$

where we have used the variance identity $\sigma^{2} = \mathbb{E}[(\mathbf{z}-\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{z}])^{2}] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{z}^{2}] - \mathbb{E}[z]^{2}$. We have assumed once more that the distributions of the weights $\Omega_{ij}$ and the hidden units $h_j$ are independent between lines three and four.

Assuming that the distribution of pre-activations $f_{j}$ at the previous layer is symmetric about zero, half of these pre-activations will be clipped by the ReLU function, and the second moment $\mathbb{E}[h_{j}^{2}]$ will be half the variance $\sigma_{f}^{2}$ of $f_{j}$ (see problem 7.14):

Appendix C.2.3

$$
\begin{aligned}
\sigma_{f_{i}^{^{\prime}}}^{2}=\sigma_{\Omega}^{^{2}}\sum_{j=1}^{D_{h}}\frac{\sigma_{f}^{^{2}}}{2}=\frac{1}{2}D_{h}\sigma_{\Omega}^{^{2}}\sigma_{f}^{^{2}}. \tag{7.31}
\end{aligned}
$$
